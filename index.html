Same but Different

A Topological Theory of Things

Zach Mainen

Every ontology begins by listing kinds of things and then describing how they relate. Objects come first; relations are secondary. TTT starts from the opposite corner: pure relationality, an undifferentiated field of difference we call the mess. No things exist yet. No boundaries, no identities, no categories. Just an endless entanglement of fibers where each fiber is a further entanglement.

From this starting point, identifiable things emerge as knots — local structures in the relational fabric that can be re-identified, counted, and compared. What makes two knots similar is their shared topology; what makes them distinct is their differing geometry. This single mechanism — topological identity with geometric variation — resolves three classical problems at once: how distinct things share characteristics, how one thing persists through change, and how knowledge can resemble its object.

The theory is parsimonious by design. Two ontological specifications — coils are plural and distinct, and coils have structure — do the work that other frameworks distribute across elaborate categorical machinery. Withdrawal is real, but it is relational, not essential. The excess of things beyond any encounter is not a hidden core but a depth of relations engaged elsewhere.
